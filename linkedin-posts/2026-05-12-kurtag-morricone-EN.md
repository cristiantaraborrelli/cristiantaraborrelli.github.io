# LinkedIn Post — 2026-05-12 (English version, Morricone + Iaquone compact + Taraborrelli explodes)
## Tipo: Portfolio
## Progetto: Fra(m)menti — Kurtág & Lorusso, Mattatoio La Pelanda Roma 2018
## Account: Cristian Taraborrelli

---

Few know that from 1965 to 1980 **Ennio Morricone** played the trumpet inside the **Gruppo di Improvvisazione Nuova Consonanza** in Rome: atonal, aleatoric music, close to noise. The other half of his biography, the one he kept far from the Oscar interviews.

That Morricone is the living inheritance of the **Associazione Nuova Consonanza**, founded in 1959 by Franco Evangelisti — the longest-running musical avant-garde in Italy. Sixty years of exposed nerves while, outside, the country changed generation three times.

In 2018, for the **55th Festival di Nuova Consonanza**, I directed and designed **Fra(m)menti** at Mattatoio La Pelanda in Rome. On stage: *Kafka-Fragmente op. 24* by **György Kurtág** — forty micro-pieces, some lasting only seconds — and *Marginalia* by **Giulia Lorusso**, in world premiere.

At my disposal, the most refined instrument of contemporary Italian theatre: the video work of **Fabio Massimo Iaquone**, pioneer of the electronic image as dramaturgical matter — author of the *DVT — Digital Versatile Theatre* concept — already at the side of Robert Wilson, of the Labèque sisters, of Giorgio Barberio Corsetti.

But an instrument is not yet a theatre. A hand is needed to tune it.

That is the part of direction I find decisive. Not *using* a language — coordinating several into one. Deciding when the image must become skin and when it must vanish; where the voice of the soprano must linger and where the violin must open into the dark; treating light, bodies, poor materials and pixels as elements of one single dramaturgy. No language is added: they are made to coincide.

What I learned in that production has shaped every project since, on entirely different scales — from opera houses to Olympic ceremonies, all the way to scenographies for contemporary fashion: the audience does not need to be told what to feel. They need a space precise enough to let attention return.

Subtraction, in a tender brief, is the most expensive thing a creative director can offer. And the only one that lasts beyond the evening.
