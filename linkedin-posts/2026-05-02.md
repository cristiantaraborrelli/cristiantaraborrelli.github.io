## Tema: Don Carlos — Teatro Mariinsky, St. Petersburg 2012

---

**TEMPVS FVGIT, AETERNITAS MANET.**

The Latin motto cut across the auto-da-fé scene of our Don Carlos at the Teatro Mariinsky in 2012. *Time flees, eternity remains.* It was the visual key of the production — and, looking back, also a way to think about what set design does inside a great opera house.

That production was Giorgio Barberio Corsetti's vision and our work together on sets and costumes — with Angela Buscemi on the costume side, Fabrice Kebour on light, Valery Gergiev on the podium. It was the first time in the Mariinsky's history that Verdi's complete five-act "Modena 1886" version was staged: four hours and ten minutes, from the winter forest of Fontainebleau to the monastery of San Giusto. And it was among the last major new productions created for the historic 1860 stage, before the Mariinsky II opened the following spring.

What I take from a project like this — and what we now bring into ceremonies, fashion-scale live events and large institutional productions — is a method: treat the stage as a place where monumental history and contemporary image can speak to each other without one cancelling the other. Video mapping next to hand-tailored costume. Inquisitorial silence next to dreaming bodies. A Latin inscription that turns the proscenium into a memento mori.

That same authorial logic is what allows a studio to move credibly from a grand opéra in St. Petersburg to a Paralympic closing ceremony in Milano Cortina, or to a fashion runway. The *language* changes. The discipline does not.
