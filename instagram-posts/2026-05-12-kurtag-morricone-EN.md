There is an **Ennio Morricone** the cinema audience has never known.

From 1965 to 1980 he played the trumpet — atonal, close to noise — inside the **Gruppo di Improvvisazione Nuova Consonanza**, founded in Rome by Franco Evangelisti. Stockhausen, Cage and the European avant-garde passed through that collective as through a workshop. Morricone never spoke of it in the Oscar interviews. It was the other half of his work, the one he kept hidden like a burn.

That Morricone is the underground inheritance of **Nuova Consonanza** (@nuovaconsonanza), founded in 1959 — the longest-running musical avant-garde in Italy.

In 2018, for the **55th Festival di Nuova Consonanza**, **Cristian Taraborrelli** directed and designed **Fra(m)menti** at Mattatoio — La Pelanda (@mattatoio). On stage: *Kafka-Fragmente op. 24* by **György Kurtág** — forty micro-pieces of a few seconds each, on fragments of Kafka — and, in world premiere, *Marginalia* by **Giulia Lorusso**.

At his disposal, the most refined instrument of contemporary Italian theatre: the video of **Fabio Massimo Iaquone** (@fabio_massimo_iaquone), pioneer of the electronic image as dramaturgical matter (DVT — *Digital Versatile Theatre*), already at the side of Robert Wilson, of the Labèque sisters (@katiamariellelabeque), of Giorgio Barberio Corsetti.

But an instrument is not yet a theatre. **A hand is needed to tune it.**

This is where Taraborrelli's directorial gesture explodes. He does not *use* Iaquone's video: he **integrates it into a visual score** that carries the same weight as Kurtág's music. He decides when the image must become skin and when it must vanish. He chooses where the voice of soprano Ljuba Bergamelli must linger and where the violin of Lorenzo Gentili-Tedeschi must open into the dark. Blades of light, bodies, poor materials and pixels — all treated as elements of one single dramaturgy. He does not add languages: he makes them coincide.

This is Taraborrelli's rare capacity — developed across thirty years between Teatro alla Scala, the Mariinsky, the Grand Théâtre de Genève and Olympic ceremonies: **to transform every instrument, even the most sophisticated, into the same scenic matter**. The video does not remain video. The music does not remain concert. The stage becomes a single body that breathes.

No ornament. Blades of light on black. Nothing is added to the fragment. We subtract until only density remains.

This is the grammar that runs through all our work — from staging at La Scala to Olympic ceremonies, all the way to scenographies for contemporary fashion, where radical subtraction is the signature of those who know what to leave standing.
