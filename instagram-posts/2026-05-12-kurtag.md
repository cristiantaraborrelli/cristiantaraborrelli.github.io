The most radical scenography is the one that knows when to disappear.

For the **55th Festival di Nuova Consonanza**, Cristian Taraborrelli directed and designed **Fra(m)menti** at Mattatoio — La Pelanda in Rome: an evening built on **György Kurtág's** *Kafka-Fragmente op. 24* (1985-87) and on **Giulia Lorusso's** *Marginalia*, premiered that same night.

Kurtág's pieces sometimes last only seconds. Forty micro-works on fragments of Kafka — prose, letters, diaries. The most radical music of the late twentieth century: maximum density of matter, minimum duration, silence weighed exactly the same as sound. The legacy of Webern, of Schumann's aphoristic flashes, of the mitteleuropean tradition that runs from Lichtenberg to Cioran.

Translating that into space meant refusing scenography as decoration. A few mobile elements. Poor materials treated as precious. Blades of light cutting the black like scalpels. The body of soprano **Ljuba Bergamelli** and the bow of violinist **Lorenzo Gentili-Tedeschi** were not framed by a set: **they were the set**, modulated by light marking time like the hands of a suspended clock. **Fabio Massimo Iaquone's** video added a fragmented digital matrix — amplification, never illustration.

The dramaturgical wager: a workshop titled *Body, Mobile Phone and Fragment*. The participants walked on stage carrying their smartphones — luminous fragments everyone keeps in a pocket — handled as **prostheses of the body**. Mobile light sources, screens that fracture the gaze, cameras that return the detail. Years before the debate became urgent in contemporary theatre: how to fold personal technology into the scene without turning it into gimmick.

The aesthetic that fashion is now asking of its runways — radical subtraction, silence as material, the spectator as part of the score — has been our grammar since this premiere.
